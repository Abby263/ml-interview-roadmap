## Start with the problem, not the model

Strong ML system design answers do not begin with model selection. They begin by clarifying the product goal, constraints, and feedback loop.

The first five minutes should answer:

- who is the user
- what action or decision the system supports
- what metric matters most
- how quickly the prediction must happen
- what data exists today

## Use a repeatable structure

A reliable answer usually moves through the same layers:

1. requirements and success metrics
2. data sources and labeling strategy
3. features or retrieval representations
4. training and evaluation
5. online serving path
6. monitoring and retraining

This structure helps you avoid wandering into model details before the interviewer trusts your framing.

## Separate offline quality from online success

Offline metrics tell you whether the model is learning the signal you want. Online metrics tell you whether the system helps the product.

For example:

- fraud detection may care about recall and false-positive rate offline
- the business may care about prevented loss, review workload, and customer friction online

## Talk about operational risk

This is where senior candidates usually differentiate themselves. Mention the parts that make production systems hard:

- delayed labels
- feature freshness
- online-offline skew
- caching trade-offs
- rollback criteria
- drift and retraining triggers

You do not need to mention every one of these. You do need to show that you know launch is not the end of the story.
